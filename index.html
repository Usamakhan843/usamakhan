Research

My doctoral research focused on solid tumour characterisation across multiple imaging modalities. The core theme is quantitative feature extraction — both handcrafted radiomic descriptors and deep feature embeddings — to support clinical decision-making, with attention to ROI integrity, feature stability, and multi-site generalisability.

Multi-centre Breast Ultrasound Classification

Ultrasound is a widely used, non-ionising modality for breast lesion assessment, but operator dependency and acquisition variability make automated classification challenging. I built end-to-end radiomics pipelines to distinguish benign from malignant lesions across three independent public datasets (BrEaST, BUID and BUS-UCLM), benchmarking pre-trained CNN and vision-transformer descriptors (DenseNet121, ConvNeXt-Base, Swin-V2-S) against conventional radiomic features. Ensemble fusion of complementary descriptors reached 81 to 82% accuracy across datasets. A systematic augmentation ablation across 14 strategies further quantified the effect of preprocessing and geometric or photometric perturbations on lesion-level classification.

Lung Nodule Classification on CT Scans

CT produces greyscale volumetric data encoding tissue density through Hounsfield units, while leading CNNs are pre-trained on natural RGB images. To bridge this gap, I investigated pseudo-colour representations of lung nodules: CT greyscale slices are mapped into three-channel colour images where each channel encodes a distinct tissue density range, enabling RGB-trained CNNs to process CT data without architectural changes. Evaluated on the LIDC-IDRI benchmark, this approach achieved 88.5% classification accuracy.

Head and Neck Cancer on FDG PET/CT

Small-volume cervical lymph nodes are frequently missed or inconsistently delineated in routine clinical practice. I contributed to FDG PET/CT radiomics studies investigating whether quantitative features can assist clinicians in identifying nodal involvement that may be overlooked by the human eye. The work included a comparative evaluation of six semi-automated tumour delineation methods on small-volume nodal lesions, and a study demonstrating that PET-derived radiomic features carry predictive value for cervical nodal status.
